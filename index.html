The perfect online course at University

Realizing that the record button wasn’t on yet, fiddling around with which screen to share and having to deal with (very) awkward silences after every question. I think I am not the only one when I say that I have experienced these situations many times already during online classes. An alternative to avoid these issues is to just follow free online courses such as Harvard’s or Udemy’s courses, where everything is very clearly structured and optimized in an online platform for years. So why shouldn’t all students just switch to professional free online courses during the pandemic? Well, an important factor to choose for regular university courses could be engagement with other students. However, this engagement will certainly be lacking due to the pandemic. This leaves us with little pros and more cons when it comes to following regular university courses during the pandemic. On the other hand, I believe that most courses can be amazingly good to follow after organizing the courses with some basic tips on online eduction.

1. Online education is boring: make it interactive and fun. Before COVID-19, an average course consisted of some reading, some lectures, talking to other students about the lecture, making a (group) assignment, studying together and then an exam. Using the same structure for an online course would definitely bore 80% of the students. That would look a bit more like reading > a lecture > more reading > another lecture > reading etc… Adding interactivity is easy nowadays. Loads of online platforms such as Zoom allow us to use a white board (or screen sharing), where teachers can walk through examples very easily. Polls, quizzes and interactivity with other students keep the 'audiance' entertained.

2. Time management and structure. A simple thing such as good time management is so important when it comes to keeping students interested. * Keep a clear overview of which online tools are used and when. If there are several tools used for communication, such as Zoom, PowerPoint, YouTube, e-mail and Canvas, it has to be clear where all the links can be found and what can be used when. The simplest document with a table of all tools and links is already enough. * Was there a plan to share your screen of an example? Then already have that example opened up, don’t let the whole class watch you struggle with finding your files. * Keeping your concentration is much harder during online lectures. If teachers are conformable enough to give live lectures, make sure to break down lectures into short understandable parts with enough breaks.

At last, as a teaching assistent, I also experienced online education from the other side. I understand that not all teachers are able and willing to be available for questions for an entire day, but students do really appreciate fast replies on emails and other questions since there is no opportunity to ask a quick question in class. Also, every student is very different. Asking for feedback from students about whether the online approach Is working with the current structure can be very helpful.

Online education can definitely be an amazing addition and brings us loads of benefits. It saves traveling time, it’s very accessible and offers flexibility. Compared to the beginning of COVID-19, I already see a difference in online courses. I think that this development of online education is very positive and I am excited for more developments in this area.
